About

I am a PhD (DPhil) student in the Department of Politics and International Relations at the University of Oxford. My research studies judicial behavior and political economy, focusing on how judges’ professional backgrounds shape the way they reason about law and regulation.

I examine whether prior legal careers—such as corporate law, prosecution, or public defense—causally influence how judges interpret cases involving crime, government regulation, and business interests. Using comparative data from the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Court of Justice of the European Union, I combine causal inference with computational analysis of judicial texts to study how professional experience shapes legal reasoning.

My doctoral studies are funded by the Clarendon Scholarship and Nuffield College. I previously completed my my master's at the University of Oxford and undergraduate at Harvard College with a degree in Government and a secondary field in Economics. Before beginning my PhD, I worked as a management consultant at Boston Consulting Group, where I worked on strategy projects for technology, financial services, and public sector organizations.

Research

  1. Berry, Joshua. Workplace Socialization and Judicial Behavior: How Professional Experience Shapes Judicial Decision-Making and Reasoning. Dissertation chapter, working paper.
    [Description]
    This chapter introduces a theory of workplace socialization explaining how judges' prior professional environments influence the information, norms, and reasoning styles they bring to interpreting and applying legal rules.
  2. Berry, Joshua. Professional Background and Criminal Law Reasoning in U.S. Courts. Dissertation chapter, working paper.
    [Description]
    Using court dockets and computational text analysis of judicial opinions, this paper examines how prosecutors and defense attorneys bring different reasoning styles to criminal law cases.
  3. Berry, Joshua. Bureaucrats and Judges: Professional Background and Administrative Law in the US, UK, and EU. Dissertation chapter, working paper.
    [Description]
    This chapter studies how judges with public- and private-sector legal careers reason about administrative law disputes, focusing on how professional experience shapes interpretations of agency authority and regulatory discretion across jurisdictions.
  4. Berry, Joshua. Elite Cues and the Durability of Free Speech Attitudes. Working paper; revising for journal submission.
    [Description]
    Using a survey experiment, this paper shows that elite messaging has limited effects on partisan attitudes toward speech regulation, suggesting that disagreements over content moderation reflect deeper ideological commitments rather than short-term cue-taking.
